3.1 Source Quality Hierarchy

Manual Section: 3.1

Category: STANDARD PRACTICE

Rationale: The four-tier source hierarchy (Primary → Corroborating → Supplementary → Excluded) follows established verification practice across intelligence community (ICD 203/206), wire services (Reuters, AFP), fact-checking networks (EFCSN), and defence intelligence (Jane’s).

Key Precedents: - ICD 203/206 establish analytic standards emphasising multi-source analysis and explicit disclosure of single-source dependence - Reuters Handbook requires minimum source standards by claim type - EFCSN Code of Standards establishes multi-source verification requirements

3.1.1 Primary Source Substitution

Manual Section: §3.1 (Primary Source Substitution)

Category: ADAPTED (75% confidence)

Rationale: The substitution rule formalises the principle that source quality can substitute for source quantity when primary sources are Grade 1 canonical instruments. A Federal Register entry, an official gazette publication, or an audited financial filing independently verifies the event it documents — its authority derives from its institutional origin, not from external corroboration. Two independent Grade 1 instruments documenting the same event provide stronger verification than one Grade 1 primary plus two Grade 3 news articles paraphrasing it.

Closest Precedents:

Framework Relevance Divergence from WEO
ICD 203 (Analytic Standards) Quality-over-quantity principle for intelligence assessments; single high-authority source can outweigh multiple low-authority sources Intelligence context, not event verification
Reuters Handbook of Journalism Convergent independent verification, not fixed source counts; authority of source weighs against number required Wire service practice, not infrastructure event verification
§5.3.4 Dimension Evidence Standard Same quality-over-quantity principle applied to structural capability claims; 1+1 base with Grade hierarchy Dimension claims, not event claims; the substitution rule extends the same logic to events

Key Design Decision:

  1. Grade 1 restriction. The substitution applies only to Grade 1 sources because their institutional authority provides self-verification. Two corporate press releases (Grade 2) or two industry analyses (Grade 3) do not carry the same self-verifying quality — they require independent corroboration to confirm accuracy. The restriction prevents the substitution from weakening the evidence standard for events where corroboration is genuinely needed.

Confidence Assessment: 75%. The principle is well-established across the precedent frameworks and is already implemented in §3.4.9 for Tier A claims (“2+ Primary OR 1P + 2C”). The substitution rule formalises and extends this existing pathway. Confidence matches §5.4.12 (Dimension Evidence Standard) because the underlying quality-over-quantity logic is identical.

Validation Commitment: Monitor whether the Grade 1 restriction is sufficient to prevent evidence-standard erosion. If events begin qualifying through the substitution pathway with sources that are technically Grade 1 but lack the self-verifying quality of canonical instruments, tighten the qualifying criteria.

3.3 Four Event Types

Manual Section: 3.6

Category: EMPIRICALLY GROUNDED (HIGH)

Rationale: The four event types (Coordination, Infrastructure, Policy, Corporate) are classified by mechanism (HOW the action occurs) and actor type (WHO performs it), not by subject matter, downstream effects, or durability.

Event Type Primary Mechanism Actor Dimension
Coordination Negotiation, mutual accommodation amongst ≥2 sovereign entities Multi-nation
Infrastructure Deployment of physical/digital systems Government/sovereign entity
Policy Unilateral legislative, regulatory, or executive action Single sovereign entity
Corporate Private sector action (partnerships, releases, market events) Private company

Key Distinction: An agreement about infrastructure = Coordination (mechanism is negotiation). A policy requiring infrastructure = Policy (mechanism is regulation). A company deploying infrastructure = Corporate (actor is private). A government deploying infrastructure = Infrastructure (actor is sovereign).

3.5 Coordination Event Criteria

Manual Section: 3.7

3.5.1 Keohane Definition

Category: EMPIRICALLY GROUNDED (VERY HIGH)

Rationale: WEO adopts Keohane’s (1984) coordination definition — observable policy adjustments through negotiation or mutual accommodation, where each entity’s actions facilitate achievement of partners’ objectives.

This definition has been IR theory consensus for 40+ years and provides clear operationalisation distinguishing: - Coordination from harmony (automatic alignment) - Coordination from parallel independent action (similar policies without negotiation) - Coordination from aspirational statements (declarations without mechanisms)

Citation: Keohane, R. O. (1984). After Hegemony: Cooperation and Discord in the World Political Economy. Princeton University Press.

3.5.2 33% Convergence Threshold

Category: EMPIRICALLY GROUNDED (HIGH)

Rationale: The approximately one-third threshold for Type 3 coordination (demonstrated convergence) derives from Finnemore & Sikkink (1998: 901), who establish that “tipping rarely occurs before one-third of the total states in the system adopt the norm.” Their analysis across women’s suffrage (1930 tipping point), the anti-personnel landmine ban (May 1997), and laws of war norms demonstrates this empirical pattern.

WEO operationalises this at 33% (~50 of ~150 AI-active nations) as a practical threshold, acknowledging this reflects an empirical approximation rather than a precise theoretical requirement.

Citation: Finnemore, M., & Sikkink, K. (1998). “International Norm Dynamics and Political Change.” International Organization 52(4): 887-917.

3.5.3 Six-Level Implementation Hierarchy

Category: EMPIRICALLY GROUNDED (VERY HIGH)

Rationale: The six-level evidence hierarchy (Announced → Adopted → Ratified → In Force → Implemented → Verified) follows Vienna Convention treaty law, OECD policy tracking standards, and SIPRI arms control verification literature.

Key Precedents: - Vienna Convention Articles 11-18 codify consent mechanisms (signature, ratification, accession); Article 24 governs entry into force - Policy monitoring frameworks distinguish announcement, adoption, and implementation phases (cf. OECD policy tracking methodology) - SIPRI Arms Control Verification hierarchy tracks commitment through operational verification

Key threshold: Binding agreements require Level 3 (ratified) minimum; non-binding frameworks require Level 5 (implemented) minimum.

3.5.4 Type 2 Level 5 — Operational Evidence Requirement

Manual Section: §3.7 (Coordination Criteria — Implementation Verification Standards)

Category: WEO SYNTHESIS (82% confidence)

Rationale: The four-pathway operational evidence framework synthesises formally codified mechanisms from multiple established governance architectures into a novel unified qualification gate. The underlying principle — that publication or catalogue adoption of an international standard does not constitute operational implementation — is formally recognised across multiple institutions: ISO/IEC Guide 21-1:2005 (Clause 1) explicitly excludes rules for the “use” of standards in production, trade, or legislation; the ISO Systematic Review (ISO/IEC Directives Part 1, Subclause 2.9) distinguishes adoption from use as separate assessment questions; and the academic decoupling literature (Boiral 2003; Aravind & Christmann 2011; Wijen 2014) provides extensive empirical documentation of the phenomenon.

The architectural precedent for maintaining independent assessment dimensions for adoption and implementation derives most directly from the FATF Mutual Evaluation Methodology (adopted February 2013, updated October 2022), which separates Technical Compliance (rules adopted into national law) from Effectiveness (rules operating in practice) using independent rating scales and evidence-based assessment across 11 Immediate Outcomes. The FATF Methodology states explicitly that the effectiveness assessment “differs fundamentally” from the assessment of technical compliance. WEO adapts this two-dimensional architectural principle from national AML/CFT compliance to individual standard implementation assessment. Each of the four evidence pathways draws on independently established institutional mechanisms: Pathway (a) on the IAF MLA Level 5 endorsement process (IAF PL 3) and ISO Survey of Certifications; Pathway (b) on the EU New Legislative Framework’s OJEU citation gate (Regulation (EU) 1025/2012, confirmed by CJEU in James Elliott Construction C-613/14 (2016) and Public.Resource.Org v Commission C-588/21 P (2024)); Pathway (c) on the ISO/IEC Directives Part 2:2021 mandatory normative reference framework (Clause 2 references “constitute requirements”); and Pathway (d) on mechanisms such as the EU Outdoor Noise Directive’s mandatory Declaration of Conformity reporting and the FATF effectiveness assessment’s evidence-of-outcomes requirement.

The novel elements are: (i) the combination of all four evidence categories into a single disjunctive qualification gate (no existing framework bundles all four); (ii) the anchoring requirement for Pathway (c), requiring normative dependency chains to terminate in a standard with independent operational evidence (no existing framework formalises this concept); and (iii) the application of this gate to individual international consensus standards as a unit of analysis for geopolitical coordination intelligence (existing analogues operate at the national regulatory system level or within single regulatory domains).

Closest Precedents:

Framework Institution Relevance Divergence from WEO
FATF Mutual Evaluation Methodology (2013/2022) FATF Strongest architectural analogue: two independent assessment dimensions (technical compliance vs. effectiveness) with separate evidence requirements and rating scales Operates at national regulatory system level, not individual standard level; uses graduated four-point scale, not binary gate; domain-specific to AML/CFT
EU New Legislative Framework (Regulation 1025/2012) European Commission / CJEU Strongest single-pathway precedent: OJEU citation as formal, judicially confirmed qualification gate between publication and regulatory operational effect Applies only to harmonised European standards within EU regulatory scope; single-dimension gate (regulatory reference only)
IAF MLA Level 5 / IAF PL 3 IAF (now GACI) Direct precedent for requiring evidence of certification activity as qualification criterion for scheme endorsement; requires “clear international, regional or national market support” and “evidence of operating” Gate operates at scheme viability level, not minimum assessment count; limited to management system and product certification standards within IAF scope
ISO/IEC Directives Part 2:2021 ISO/IEC Direct codification of normative dependency mechanism — normative references “constitute requirements” Does not formalise anchored vs. unanchored chains; no operational evidence requirement for referenced standards
Basel Committee RCAP BCBS Three-tier assessment approach (adoption timeliness → transposition consistency → bank-level outcomes) confirming institutional recognition of the adoption/implementation gap Explicitly acknowledges it does not assess supervisory effectiveness; banking regulation only

Key Design Decisions:

  1. Disjunctive gate (any one pathway sufficient). Unlike FATF’s 11 Immediate Outcomes (all assessed), WEO requires only one of four pathways. This reflects the binary nature of the implementation question for individual standards: the question is whether ANY operational effect exists, not a comprehensive assessment of implementation depth.

  2. Pathway (c) anchoring. A normative dependency chain cannot be self-referencing without an anchor. At least one standard in the chain must independently satisfy Pathway (a), (b), or (d). This prevents a cluster of published-but-unimplemented standards from bootstrapping each other to Level 5 through circular normative references. No existing framework formalises this concept.

  3. Binary gate vs. graduated scale. WEO uses pass/fail rather than FATF’s graduated four-point scale. This is a deliberate design choice — WEO needs a classification decision (implemented or not), not an implementation quality assessment. The EU NLF’s OJEU citation gate provides direct precedent for binary treatment in standards governance.

  4. Retrospective application. The operational evidence requirement applies to all events in the database, not only to new candidates. Standards that entered under V1.2 criteria where publication alone satisfied Level 5 are assessed against the four pathways. Events failing all four pathways are removed from the database. This ensures the database reflects verified operational coordination, not catalogued intentions.

Confidence Assessment: 82%. The principle that publication does not equal implementation is formally codified in multiple institutions (ISO/IEC Guide 21-1, FATF Methodology, EU NLF), and three of the four pathways have direct or strong analogous institutional precedent. Confidence is bounded below 90% because: (a) no single framework bundles all four pathways, making this a synthesis rather than an adaptation; (b) the anchoring requirement for Pathway (c) is genuinely novel; and (c) the application to individual standards as a unit of analysis for geopolitical intelligence has no direct precedent. Confidence is above 75% because the architectural principle is well-established, the FATF analogue is strong, and each pathway independently maps onto formally codified mechanisms.

Validation Commitment: Monitor for (1) any ISO or IEC governance reform that creates a formal implementation tracking mechanism for the full standards catalogue; (2) evolution of the merged IAF/ILAC entity (Global Accreditation Cooperation Inc.) and any changes to scheme endorsement criteria; (3) academic literature proposing multi-pathway implementation assessment frameworks; (4) FATF 5th Round methodology updates that may refine effectiveness assessment criteria; and (5) any extension of the EU NLF gate mechanism to non-harmonised standards.

3.6.2 Option E — Corporate Strategic Disclosure

Manual Section: §3.5, Stage 2 Option E Category: ADAPTED (HIGH)

Rationale: Option E adapts established regulatory evidentiary practice to the Stage 2 AI connection question for Corporate events. Corporate financial instruments — PPAs, equity agreements, procurement contracts — specify capacity, price, and delivery terms without designating workload type. This is structurally parallel to the gaps addressed by PIET (export controls list by HS code) and OEP (infrastructure documentation is application-agnostic): the instrument class does not produce AI-specific language by convention.

The evidentiary approach — accepting regulated corporate disclosure as evidence of the strategic purpose of a transaction whose operative instrument is silent on that purpose — has well-established precedent across international regulatory frameworks. Five of seven frameworks examined make the approach mandatory or statutory; no framework rejects it in principle.

Framework Established Practice
US Antitrust (HSR Act Items 4(c)/4(d); 2023 Merger Guidelines) Mandatory filing of strategic analyses; ordinary-course documents more probative than retrospective materials
EU Merger Control (Form CO §5.4; Facebook/WhatsApp €110M fine) Mandatory filing of board presentations; regulated disclosures treated as binding representations

Option E’s requirements implement five convergent safeguards identified across all examined frameworks: regulated filing status, contemporaneity, transaction specificity, senior authorisation, and corroboration. The 1+1 evidence structure (regulated disclosure + corroborating signal) is proportionate to the lower underlying uncertainty — PIET and OEP address cases where the AI connection is genuinely ambiguous, whilst Option E addresses cases where the implementing entity acknowledges AI purpose in regulated disclosure. The calibration principle follows Philip Morris v. Australia (ICSID): evidentiary thresholds proportionate to the consequence of the determination.

Documented limitation: Japan – Alcoholic Beverages II (WT/DS8/AB/R, 1996) rejected subjective intent inquiry. Option E focuses on operational function (role in AI infrastructure), not subjective corporate intent — consistent with the “design, architecture, and structure” framework that also underpins the T2 Impact-Demonstrated mechanism (§3.7.4).

Confidence Assessment: HIGH. Five of seven frameworks make the approach mandatory or statutory. The adaptation to WEO’s Stage 2 classification context is a bounded domain transfer — the evidentiary logic is established; the application to AI connection determination is the contribution.

3.6.3 Option F — Defence Appropriation Evidence

Manual Section: §3.5, Stage 2 Option F Category: ADAPTED (MEDIUM-HIGH)

Rationale: Policy events with genuine AI connections that are funded through defence appropriation processes face a structural documentation constraint that is universal, not specific to WEO. Defence appropriation documents fund AI-specific programmes through publicly verifiable financial instruments. Once appropriated funds enter implementation, operational detail moves behind classification barriers — a permanent feature of military spending architecture across all major defence economies. The appropriation stage is therefore the sole publicly verifiable evidence window for the AI connection.

Any framework attempting to track military AI activity from public documentation encounters the same structural barrier: the evidence required for post-implementation verification does not exist in publicly accessible form. The constraint is empirically documented across five surveyed economies and is consistent with the broader finding that observable documentation surface contracts during re-militarisation whilst underlying activity expands (§4.11).

Option F accepts binding defence financial documents as AI connection evidence at Stage 2, capturing events at the sole point where the AI connection is publicly documented. The adaptation applies existing primary source evidentiary standards — enacted legislation and executive budget submissions qualify as Tier A evidence per §3.4.4 — to a specific document class not previously recognised in the AI Connection Filter’s option structure.

Boundary logic — eligibility by Event Type and document class:

The classification barrier affects all event types within the defence domain. However, existing pathways already serve Coordination and Infrastructure events:

  • Coordination events in the defence context (e.g., multilateral defence AI partnerships) typically qualify via Keohane auto-qualification because the agreement or communiqué itself is public and contains AI-specific language. Classification constrains downstream implementation detail, not the coordination event’s qualification evidence.
  • Infrastructure events in the defence context (e.g., classified military AI facilities) are served by OEP (Option D), which establishes AI connection through operational physical evidence where official documentation is absent or application-agnostic.
  • Policy events funded through defence appropriations have no fallback pathway when Options A, B, and C fail. These are the events where the appropriation document is the sole primary source evidence of AI connection.

Option F fills this remaining gap. Eligibility is determined by Event Type (Policy, established at Stage 1) and document class (defence appropriation documents), consistent with how Options D, E, and PIET scope their eligibility. Domain classification is a separate, independent assessment at Stage 4.

3.12.1 Universal Membership Categories

Manual Section: §3.10.1 (category definitions)

Category: ADAPTED (75% confidence)

Source methodology: Systematic survey of eight international tiered classification frameworks: NATO partnership tiers, EU accession framework, FATF grey/black list classification, OECD accession framework, Basel Committee RCAP, Shanghai Cooperation Organisation membership tiers, Non-Aligned Movement (historical), and contemporary technology alliance frameworks (Pax Silica, Chip 4, AISI Network, Wassenaar Arrangement).

Adaptation rationale: No existing framework offers a symmetric, universal set of membership categories applied identically to competing technology ecosystems. Every surveyed operational framework is anchored by one hegemon, with rivals as residual or excluded. The five-category universal structure (Core / Integrated / Engaged / Peripheral / Non-Aligned) is WEO’s synthesis of transferable design primitives from these frameworks, adapted for technology ecosystem alignment classification.

Key design decisions:

  1. Five-category count. The modal tier count across the eight surveyed frameworks is five ± one. NATO functionally operates with three sub-member tiers plus the member tier; OECD maintains five tiers; EU has four substantive tiers. Five categories provides sufficient discrimination to capture the spectrum from structural embedding (US in Western ecosystem, 7/7 SCP dimensions as of May 2026) to single-vector connection (e.g., governance participation without infrastructure dependency) without classification noise from finer subdivision.

  2. Strength over mechanism. Categories describe alignment strength (how deeply integrated), not alignment mechanism (how the nation entered the ecosystem). This follows Basel RCAP’s design — the only surveyed framework that cleanly separates membership status from compliance assessment. The mechanism (formal agreement, voluntary technology choice, infrastructure lock-in, political alliance, governance participation) is recorded as a descriptive attribute within each nation’s register entry. This resolves the V1.3 asymmetry where Western categories described mechanisms (Conditional, Functional, Governance) and Eastern categories described dependency types (Aligned, Dependent, Leaning).

3.12.1a Bloc Assignment Gate

Manual Section: §3.10.1 (Bloc Assignment Gate subsection)

Category: ADAPTED (75% confidence)

Rationale: The two-track bloc assignment gate fills the architectural gap between the general classification principle (“every nation is classified by which ecosystem its AI infrastructure primarily operates within”) and the within-bloc category assignment. In V1.3, Non-Aligned actors had a formalised identification mechanism (the primary partner test: financial backing → technology transfer → strategic initiative leadership), but actors assigned to the Western or Eastern blocs had no equivalently formalised assignment process. The bloc assignment was qualitatively sound but procedurally implicit.

Track 1 formalises the observable infrastructure alignment assessment that was already being applied in practice. The three indicators (hardware origin, compute/model platforms, regulatory alignment) are the same signals that informed every existing Bloc Membership Register entry. Track 2 extends the assessment to the full seven-dimension dependency direction analysis for contested cases where the observable indicators are ambiguous or contradictory. The seven-dimension framework draws directly from the Sovereign Capability Profiles (§5.3.2), applied here as a dependency direction test rather than a capability assessment — asking “whose ecosystem does this actor draw on?” rather than “what does this actor independently control?”

The dead heat rule (exact dependency split = Non-Aligned) follows from the operational definition of Non-Aligned status: the absence of predominant alignment to either ecosystem. If a structured dependency analysis across all seven dimensions produces an exact split, the actor has demonstrated through its infrastructure choices that it is not structurally embedded in either ecosystem — which is precisely what Non-Aligned status describes. Event-level attribution for such actors uses the existing three-priority hierarchy (§3.12.3).

The near-dead-heat rule (narrow majority constrains category to Peripheral or Engaged) ensures that the category assignment reflects the strength of structural embedding. An actor with a 4–3 dependency split has a measurably weaker ecosystem attachment than an actor with a 7–0 or 6–1 split. Assigning such an actor to Core or Integrated would overstate the depth of its embedding.

The four override conditions ensure the tiebreaker adapts to cases where the default ordering breaks down.

3.12.1.3 Third Bloc Formation — Transitional Classification

Manual Section: §3.10.1.3

Category: WEO SYNTHESIS (65% confidence)

Rationale: The two-bloc structure (Western, Eastern) reflects the empirical reality at V1.4 publication — only two actors satisfy the PAA qualification threshold (§5.3). However, the methodology must accommodate the possibility of a third bloc forming without requiring architectural revision. The most likely formation scenario involves an actor currently classified within an existing bloc approaching PAA qualification through independent sovereign capability accumulation across multiple SCP dimensions, causing a structural split between the new and original blocs.

The structural problem: If a third bloc forms from within an existing bloc, nations previously classified as Core in the original bloc would not cleanly transfer to the new bloc. Their ecosystem dependencies would be split — some dimensions aligning with the new bloc (e.g. regulation, investment, equipment), others remaining with the original (e.g. chip design, compute, models). This is structurally different from Non-Aligned status, which reflects strategic choice or neutrality. Split-alignment during bloc formation reflects unavoidable structural transition where the infrastructure dependencies themselves are divided.

Transitional mechanism rationale: Reclassifying structurally split nations as Non-Aligned during the transition period — and applying the three-priority hierarchy (§3.12.3) per-event — repurposes an existing, tested mechanism for a new scenario. This avoids the need for a new classification framework whilst providing event-level accuracy. The three-priority test is well-suited because it assesses each event individually against measurable criteria (financial backing, critical technology, strategic leadership), which correctly handles the situation where a nation’s events may genuinely belong to different blocs depending on which ecosystem capabilities are utilised.

Stabilisation criterion rationale: “Predominance across the seven SCP dimensions” rather than a simple dimensional count (e.g. 4/7) was chosen because dimensions are not equally weighted in practice. Hardware dependencies (D1–D3) create deeper structural lock-in than regulatory alignment (D6), which can shift with a single legislative act. The stabilisation assessment must consider the structural weight of dependencies, not merely count them. This judgement is left to the analyst rather than algorithmically specified, consistent with the indicator-based assessment approach used throughout §3.10.1.

Category accommodation rationale: During the transition period, the nation is classified as Non-Aligned and events are attributed per the three-priority hierarchy. The analyst tracks the dependency pattern across both blocs to assess when stabilisation occurs. Upon reclassification to a specific bloc, the category level reflects the strength of alignment at that point — a nation emerging from transition is unlikely to qualify as Core immediately, as residual dependencies on the former bloc would typically place it at Engaged or Peripheral. The expectation that alignment will stabilise over time is grounded in the economic logic of supply chain consolidation — maintaining parallel dependencies across two ecosystems is costly and strategically unstable.

Scope limitation rationale: Full multi-bloc methodology (formal dimensional dependency assessment, weighted scoring) is deferred rather than attempted. The transitional mechanism is sufficient for initial classification and avoids over-engineering a solution for a scenario that has not yet occurred. If a third bloc forms, the specific dimensional profile of the new PAA and the specific dependencies of affected nations will inform the full methodology — designing it in the abstract risks producing a framework mismatched to the actual scenario.

Confidence: 65%. This is the minimum confidence threshold (§2.3). The transitional mechanism is logically sound and repurposes tested components (Non-Aligned classification + three-priority test), but it has not been empirically tested against a real third-bloc formation scenario. Confidence reflects this untested status. Reassess when a third PAA candidate approaches qualification.

4.6.3 F3 Pathway Exemption

Manual Section: §4.9, Factor 3 (F3) — pathway exemption

Category: WEO SYNTHESIS (75% confidence)

Rationale: F3 filters general technology and digital economy policies where AI is mentioned incidentally (data protection law with an automated decision-making article, copyright directive with a text-mining exception). Its structural indicators — AI in policy title, AI-specific definitions, AI-specific obligations — are calibrated to regulatory and governance instruments where the legislature chooses whether to make AI the primary subject.

Two AI Connection pathways (Option F and Option G) serve event classes where the legislature structurally cannot make AI the primary subject because the instrument addresses the programme or the material — not the downstream application. Defence appropriation acts fund entire departmental budgets; critical minerals acts address mineral supply chains. In both cases, the AI infrastructure relevance is real and documentable, but the instrument’s structural format guarantees F3 failure regardless of the event’s genuine significance.

The pathway exemption resolves this by recognising that each pathway’s own evidence requirement performs the AI centrality function more precisely than F3’s generic indicators. Option F requires explicit AI designation in programme titles within the financial instrument. Option G requires documented causal chains from the controlled material to AI infrastructure applications. Each test is more demanding and more specific than F3’s title-or-definitions check.

Evidence basis: Two gap reports — GAP-2026-04-14-001 (Option F, April 2026) and GAP-2026-05-13-001 (supply-chain response events, May 2026) — separately documented the same structural barrier. The §4.9 F3 coverage note acknowledges the pattern: “Application-agnostic Policy instruments controlling physical materials or technology products… will typically fail F3 because the instrument’s primary subject is the controlled material or technology, not AI.” The pathway exemption implements what the coverage note identified: each qualifying pathway’s own evidence standard performs the AI centrality function more precisely than F3’s generic indicators.

Pass logic adjustment: For exempt events, PISA-5 is assessed on four factors (F1, F2, F4, F5). Auto-qualification requires ≥3/4 factors. The F1+F2 double-fail exclusion rule is preserved — neither significant jurisdiction nor regulatory scope remains grounds for automatic disqualification regardless of F3 status. The standard PISA-5 has a 60% auto-qualify route (F1+F2+F3) that depends on F3’s mandatory-gate status; with F3 removed, the lowest auto-qualify ratio rises from 60% to 75% (≥3/4), an appropriate tightening given the narrower factor set. Marginal cases (exactly 2/4 with F1+F2 not both failing) route to Human Review under the existing TB1–TB5 tiebreaker framework.

Confidence: 75%. The structural mismatch is documented in two separate gap reports. The resolution is logically sound (pathway evidence subsumes F3’s function). The 65% minimum threshold (§2.3) is met. Confidence is bounded below 80% because the exemption has not yet been applied to live events — first application will occur following V1.4 publication.

Validation commitment: Monitor F3-exempt events for quality. If events qualifying through pathway exemption are systematically lower quality than standard PISA-5 events, reassess whether the pathway evidence requirements are sufficiently demanding.

Manual Section: §5.3 (Principal AI Actors)

Category: CATEGORY CREATOR (75% confidence)

However, the substantive theoretical architecture underlying WEO’s infrastructure-based determination is present across multiple leading institutions, which have constructed analogous full-stack technological typologies using identical supply-chain chokepoints to define sovereign AI power.

Key Design Decisions:

  1. Hardware stack disaggregation. No existing framework treats accelerator design, semiconductor fabrication, semiconductor equipment, and cloud compute as four separate, co-equal dimensions. Brookings bundles hardware under “compute”; Chatham House uses “compute” as a single primary building block. WEO disaggregates because supply-chain chokepoint analysis requires dimensional granularity — a nation can dominate fabrication (Taiwan) without designing accelerators, or dominate equipment (Netherlands) without fabricating chips. Bundling obscures the distribution of supply-chain power that WEO’s bloc classification methodology is designed to track.

  2. Dimensional scope: sovereign control over AI infrastructure outputs. WEO’s seven SCP dimensions — AI accelerator design, advanced semiconductor fabrication, semiconductor manufacturing equipment, cloud AI compute services, frontier AI model development, AI-specific regulatory authority with extraterritorial reach, and sovereign AI infrastructure investment at scale — measure assets, instruments, and capabilities that a sovereign actor can independently control and that determine which AI infrastructure ecosystem the actor operates within. This scope is more focused than comprehensive AI power frameworks (Brookings, Chatham House, IISS, Belfer), which additionally assess variables such as talent, data, and energy. WEO’s dimensional focus reflects the specific analytical question the SCP framework is designed to answer: not how much AI capability does this actor possess in aggregate, but what does this actor independently control in the AI infrastructure supply chain, and which ecosystem does that place it within? Variables that contribute to AI capability broadly but do not independently determine ecosystem position — including workforce, training data, and energy capacity — are not assessed as SCP dimensions. These variables do appear in WEO’s event-level analysis where they constitute characteristics of AI infrastructure coordination activity: energy through the Energy/Infrastructure domain and Environmental Nexus Tags framework, data governance through policy events, and workforce provisions through event characteristics such as CHIPS Act award conditions. The dimensional exclusion is a scope decision at the actor-assessment level, not an analytical judgement about the importance of these variables to AI development. This focused scope, combined with the three-gate designation structure (§5.4.2), produces materially different actor classifications from broader frameworks: most notably, the EU scores 4/7 on WEO’s dimensional assessment (including regulatory authority and sovereign investment) but does not qualify as a principal AI actor because its hardware dimensions cannot be sustained independently under the severance test — a structural dependency that broader typologies such as Brookings’ “Broad Stack Leaders” do not assess.

WEO uses a binary threshold (≥3 of 7 dimensions) producing a discrete “principal actor / not principal actor” determination. This follows the methodological pattern established by the Basel G-SIB methodology (already adapted for WEO’s Basel 5-Factor significance assessment): multi-factor assessment producing a discrete designation that triggers specific regulatory consequences. T2 Scope: Principal Actor Requirement Not Extended

Manual Section: §5.3 (T2 scope paragraph)

Category: WEO SYNTHESIS (70% confidence)

Rationale: The principal actor requirement is structurally specific to T1. T1 measures ecosystem-level integration — whether distinct AI ecosystems are operationally aligned through verified cross-bloc adoption or implementation by their respective principal AI actors. Without ecosystem controllers at the table, bilateral contacts between non-principal members from different blocs do not bridge the two ecosystems. T2 measures a different dynamic: structural separation between blocs. The significance of between-bloc fragmentation depends on the structural effect — what is disrupted, displaced, or barred — rather than the identity of the actor creating the separation.

This asymmetry is grounded in two independently established theoretical frameworks. Hegemonic Stability Theory (Kindleberger, 1973; Keohane, 1984; Gilpin, 1981) establishes that systemic cooperation requires hegemonic leadership to overcome collective action problems — Keohane’s explicit disconfirmation condition makes actor identity a necessary condition for systemically significant cooperation. Weaponized Interdependence (Farrell & Newman, 2019) establishes that network fragmentation depends on structural position (control of hubs and chokepoints), not on state power or hegemonic status — the chokepoint effect is inherently actor-agnostic. Hirshleifer’s aggregation technology framework (1983) provides the formal bridge: best-shot public goods (cooperation outcomes) require at least one highly capable actor, whilst weakest-link public goods (disruption vulnerability) are determined by the least capable or most disruptive node.

The asymmetry holds across all three T2 fragmentation types:

  • Restriction: Supply-chain cuts can originate from any node with sufficient leverage. Critical supply-chain nodes command chokepoint positions (Netherlands: ~100% EUV lithography; Japan: critical deposition, etching, metrology equipment; Taiwan: ~90% advanced logic fabrication) where unilateral action by a non-principal actor produces fragmentation effects equivalent to or exceeding direct principal actor action. The T2 Restriction tests (Explicit-Targeting and PIET Impact-Demonstrated) measure the effect through named other-bloc targeting, trade flow disruption (>50%/>90%), price impacts (>20%), and government policy responses (≥2).
  • Buildout: Parallel infrastructure development creates structural separation through the existence of alternative capacity, not through the identity of the builder. The Basel 5-Factor framework ($100M floor, cross-jurisdictional significance, substitutability assessment) directly measures whether the buildout creates structurally significant parallel capacity.
  • Mandatory Standard: Regulatory barriers create fragmentation through enforcement scope and compliance burden, not through the identity of the legislating entity. The EU currently exercises regulatory authority with extraterritorial reach without qualifying as a principal AI actor under the §5.3 dimensional assessment. The Mandatory Standard Test (mandatory language + documented enforcement) measures the structural barrier directly.

Clayton, Maggiori & Schreger (2024) provide the closest single-framework articulation: their model of economic coercion and fragmentation mathematically demonstrates that global integration is driven by hegemonic coordination of externalities (supporting T1’s actor requirement), whilst fragmentation is driven by uncoordinated, structurally-determined pursuit of economic security by actors at any scale (supporting T2’s effect-based approach).

5.4.1 Dimension Qualification Principles

Manual Section: §5.3.2 (Dimension Qualification Principles)

Category: CATEGORY CREATOR (75% confidence)

Rationale: The parent PAA framework (§5.3, 7-dimension, ≥3 threshold) is documented as CATEGORY CREATOR at 75% confidence in Rationale §5.4. The per-dimension qualification principles operationalise the existing “dominant or independently significant capability” language with specific definitions that resolve edge cases at the boundary of each dimension. No existing geopolitical or technology governance framework publishes explicit per-dimension qualification thresholds for AI infrastructure capability assessment. The frameworks surveyed for §5.4 (IISS Cyber Capabilities, Brookings “Broad Stack Leaders,” Belfer CET Index, Chatham House AI Building Blocks, Basel G-SIB) all assess analogous dimensions but none provide the definitional specificity required for reproducible binary classification.

Key Design Decisions:

  1. Qualification principles as sentences, not quantitative thresholds. Each dimension’s qualification principle is expressed as a definitional sentence rather than a numerical threshold (e.g., market share percentage, revenue floor, benchmark percentile). This choice trades precision for durability: the AI infrastructure landscape evolves rapidly, and quantitative thresholds calibrated to 2026 market conditions would require constant recalibration. Definitional sentences permit consistent application across assessment cycles whilst accommodating landscape evolution.

  2. Subsidiary clause harmonisation. A uniform subsidiary/acquisition clause is applied across Dimensions 1, 3, 4, and 5 — the four dimensions where corporate entities may be controlled from a different territory than their physical operations. This addresses edge cases (Habana Labs/Israel, Graphcore/UK, DeepMind/UK) where corporate control and physical operations diverge. The clause follows the same logic as Dimension 5’s existing subsidiary treatment, extended for cross-dimensional consistency.

    Sensitivity note: The subsidiary and acquisition clause affects the distribution of PAA scores across actors. Attribution of subsidiary lab outputs to the parent entity’s territory (e.g., DeepMind → United States via Alphabet) concentrates Dimension 5 credits in actors hosting parent companies rather than those hosting research operations. As of V1.4 publication (May 2026), no actor would cross the ≥3/≥2-categories threshold under an alternative attribution rule granting strategic autonomy to subsidiary labs — this finding should be reassessed at each methodology version milestone, particularly as new actors develop frontier AI capabilities through subsidiary or acquisition structures.

  3. Subsystem inclusion in Dimension 3. The equipment dimension extends to “sole-sourced subsystems and components essential to the functioning” of critical equipment, not only complete equipment systems. This covers the Zeiss SMT case (sole-source EUV lithography optics, without which no advanced fab can operate) that four independent AI research inputs unanimously identified as structurally necessary. The “sole-sourced” and “essential to functioning” qualifiers prevent the inclusion of commodity components.

  4. Sovereign control principle generalisation (Dimension 2 → all physical infrastructure dimensions). The sovereign control principle applies territorial presence as the primary test for all physical infrastructure dimensions (D2 fabrication, D4 compute), reflecting the physical reality that these capabilities are geographically bound — a fab cannot be relocated, and its output serves the territory where it operates regardless of corporate ownership. D4 additionally requires both domestic HQ and territorial compute presence (see key design decision 8 below). Corporate capability dimensions (D1 accelerator design, D5 model development) are assessed by ownership chain — the corporate parent’s territory has effective control through strategic direction and IP ownership. D3 (equipment) applies both tests. See key design decisions 7–8 below for the sovereign control principle’s full specification and the D4 territorial compute requirement.

  5. Dimension 2/3 process-node threshold recalibration. Dimension 2 uses a quantitative process-node threshold (≤7nm) as the boundary for “advanced” fabrication — an exception to the general design preference for definitional sentences over quantitative thresholds (see point 1 above). This threshold reflects the assessment-date definition of advanced semiconductor manufacturing and is subject to periodic recalibration as the global fabrication frontier advances. The threshold should be reviewed at each methodology version milestone against the current state of leading-edge production. The recalibration trigger is when the specified threshold ceases to discriminate between actors with frontier or near-frontier fabrication capability and those operating at mature commodity nodes — operationally, when ≥4 actors can independently manufacture at the threshold node without structural equipment dependencies. Recalibration adjusts the threshold downward (e.g., from ≤7nm to ≤5nm or ≤3nm) to maintain analytical discrimination. This recalibration applies equally to Dimension 3, which references ≤7nm in its equipment substitutability test — when the Dimension 2 threshold is recalibrated, Dimension 3 must follow to maintain cross-dimensional consistency.

  6. Dimension 5 benchmark saturation monitoring. The Dimension 5 qualification principle relies on “broadly recognised evaluation benchmarks” as external instruments to discriminate between frontier and non-frontier models. These benchmarks saturate over time — a benchmark on which the top 20 models score within 1–2% of each other no longer discriminates meaningfully. Unlike the ≤7nm threshold, this is not a quantitative anchor requiring recalibration but a dependency on external measurement instruments whose discriminating power degrades. The methodology does not prescribe specific benchmarks (this is deliberate — benchmark relevance evolves faster than methodology versions), but assessors should document which benchmarks informed each assessment cycle’s D5 verdicts. When the benchmarks available at an assessment date can no longer clearly separate “top tier” from the broader field, this should be flagged as a reduced-confidence assessment rather than treated as an expansion of the qualifying set.

Additional Qualification Principles

  1. Sovereign control principle (governing rule). Physical infrastructure dimensions (D2 fabrication, D4 compute) are assessed by territorial presence — the sovereign territory where infrastructure physically operates has effective control. Corporate capability dimensions (D1 accelerator design, D5 model development) are assessed by ownership chain — the corporate parent’s territory has effective control through strategic direction and IP ownership. D3 (equipment) applies both tests: the entity must be headquartered in the actor’s territory AND control the qualifying technology. D6–D7 (governance) are inherently sovereign. Precedent basis: International investment law (territorial jurisdiction vs nationality of investment in BIT framework), OECD permanent establishment doctrine, WTO rules of origin, CFIUS screening rationale. Establishing cases: Netherlands D4 (Nebius — Dutch corporate domicile, zero Dutch-territory compute → NOT MET); Taiwan D1 (MediaTek — Taiwanese firm, client retains architectural IP under design-service model → NOT MET under ownership chain test); US D2 (TSMC Arizona — Taiwanese-owned fab on US soil → MET under territorial test).

  2. D4 territorial compute requirement. “Domestically controlled” in D4 requires operational compute on the actor’s territory or under the actor’s direct sovereign jurisdiction, not merely corporate domicile of the parent entity. Corporate ownership of compute infrastructure entirely abroad does not satisfy D4. Both D2 and D4 assess physical infrastructure; the sovereign control principle applies the same territorial presence test to both. Establishing case: Netherlands / Nebius Group N.V. — Dutch-domiciled company with zero qualifying compute on Dutch soil; all operational compute located in Finland (Mäntsälä). D4 NOT MET for the Netherlands.

  3. D6 dual-pathway clarification. The Dimension 6 qualification principle covers both AI-specific legislation and general-purpose regulatory or trade control frameworks whose primary documented application targets AI systems, AI compute hardware, AI models, or AI-relevant manufacturing equipment. Export control regimes with documented extraterritorial enforcement qualify alongside dedicated AI-specific regulatory frameworks. This clarification ensures that actors exercising AI-relevant extraterritorial authority through trade controls (e.g., US export controls on advanced semiconductors and AI chips) are assessed equivalently to actors exercising authority through dedicated AI legislation (e.g., EU AI Act).

  4. D7 national proportionality standard. “Nationally transformative” in Dimension 7 is assessed relative to the actor’s own GDP, fiscal capacity, and industrial base — not against a global spending threshold. A modest absolute spend that represents a structural reorientation of a smaller actor’s industrial policy may satisfy the dimension, whilst a large absolute spend by a major economy that represents marginal budgetary commitment may not. Establishing case: Netherlands — modest absolute spend relative to US or China, but representing fundamental reorientation of Dutch industrial and technology policy towards sovereign AI infrastructure capability.

  5. D4 assessment-date-relative threshold. The D4 hyperscale threshold (“sufficient to train a current-generation frontier foundation model”) is assessment-date-relative. “Current-generation frontier” shifts as frontier training scales up; no fixed GPU count is prescribed. At each assessment cycle, the threshold is calibrated against the most recent verified frontier training runs. Clusters significantly below the prevailing frontier training scale are clearly NOT MET; clusters at or above are clearly MET; actors between the two require case-specific evidence (empirical frontier-class training run, or demonstrable technical configuration showing frontier training feasibility). Establishing cases: South Korea NOT MET at ~4,000 B200 GPUs, Taiwan NOT MET at ~4,608 GB200, Germany NOT MET at ~10,000 Blackwell (~0.5 ExaFLOPS vs ~5+ ExaFLOPS required), India NOT MET at 16,384 H100 (empirical training run produced sub-frontier model).

  6. D7 general-infrastructure-fund exclusion. General infrastructure funds not earmarked for AI or technology do not satisfy Dimension 7. The dimension assesses sovereign AI infrastructure investment, not aggregate government capital expenditure. Attribution of a country’s entire infrastructure or defence budget to D7 inflates the figure and misrepresents the sovereign AI commitment. Establishing case: Germany — €500B Special Fund for Infrastructure and Climate Neutrality is a general infrastructure instrument, not creditable to D7.

  7. D7 announced-vs-executed investment. Government adoption of committee recommendations or announcement of national project budgets does not satisfy D7 if execution data shows systematic under-delivery. The dimension requires evidence of actually appropriated and disbursed capital, not aspirational targets. Announced envelopes that have been systematically under-executed do not meet the “at scale” threshold. Establishing case: Russia — announced budgets exceeded executed spending by 5–20× across multiple budget cycles; Data Economy national project 11.2% executed by March 2025.

  8. D5 single-domain exclusion. Frontier-competitive performance in a single capability domain (e.g., image generation, speech recognition, code) does not satisfy D5’s “≥3 benchmarks across capability domains” requirement. The dimension tests general-purpose frontier capability, not domain-specific excellence. Establishing case: Germany — Black Forest Labs FLUX.2 is frontier in image generation but single-domain only.

  9. D2 marketing-node exclusion. Semiconductor process nodes are assessed by their lithographic and transistor-density characteristics, not marketing labels. Intel’s rebranded node names (Intel 7, Intel 4, Intel 3) are mapped to their actual technology class for assessment purposes. Intel 7 is assessed as 10nm-class (DUV, no EUV) and does not qualify for the ≤7nm threshold. Establishing case: Israel — Intel Fab 28 at Kiryat Gat operates Intel 7 / 10nm Enhanced SuperFin.

  10. Synthesis majority override rule. A nominal model majority in the synthesis process can be overridden when the majority’s interpretive framework contradicts its own cited evidence. The factual base must be agreed across models; the override applies on interpretive grounds only, where the majority draws a conclusion inconsistent with the evidence those same models cite. Establishing cases: France D5 (2-1 MET overridden — 44% GPQA Diamond vs 91–94% frontier is not “top tier” by any defensible reading); Russia D7 (2-1 MET overridden — announced budgets under-executed by 5–20×).

  11. D3 subsidiary clause. The D3 dual test (HQ in territory + technology control) means foreign-parented subsidiaries with local manufacturing are assessed under the parent’s territory, consistent with the subsidiary clause. This is distinct from D2, where the territorial presence exception credits any fab on an actor’s soil regardless of ownership. D3 requires the qualifying entity to be headquartered in the actor’s territory; a US-parented subsidiary manufacturing in Canada credits to the US, not Canada. Establishing case: Canada — Teledyne DALSA (Teledyne Technologies subsidiary, US parent) manufactures EUV pellicle membranes at Bromont, QC; credits to US D3, not Canadian D3. Consistent with exclusion of Applied Materials Israel and KLA Israel from Israel’s D3.

  12. Actor Designation Framework temporal disclaimer. Designation scores are assessment-date-stamped snapshots of current capabilities. They are not retrospective assessments of capabilities at the time each event in the database occurred. Bloc classifications for individual events were assigned under the methodology version current at the time of entry. The Actor Designation Framework does not retroactively alter event-level classifications made under prior methodology versions. This distinction is documented in the platform interface alongside the designation display.

Evidence Basis: Dimension qualification principles were calibrated through multi-agent independent AI research synthesis — a methodology necessitated by the absence of historical empirical data or established expert consensus for sovereign AI infrastructure capability assessment at dimension-level granularity. Multiple independent AI research inputs assessed each actor against each dimension independently; a dedicated synthesis process consolidated convergences, resolved divergences, and applied the majority override rule (addition 16 above) where interpretive disagreement conflicted with cited evidence.

Confidence Assessment: 75%. Inherits from the parent PAA framework (75%) with calibration refinements from Batch 1 actor assessments. 5.4.2 Three-Gate PAA Designation, Severance Test, and AIK Classification

Manual Section: §5.3.1 (Principal AI Actor Designation: Three-Gate Structure)

Category: CATEGORY CREATOR (70–75% confidence)

Rationale: A capability threshold alone (≥3 of 7 dimensions) would conflate capability breadth with ecosystem independence. As additional actors accumulate capability breadth whilst remaining structurally dependent on an existing PAA’s ecosystem for critical inputs, the threshold must be supplemented by a resilience assessment. Without the severance test, the designation would potentially classify Japan (deeply embedded in the US-centred ecosystem despite significant sovereign capabilities) as an ecosystem anchor equivalent to the United States. The three-gate structure separates capability (Gate 1), independence (Gate 2), and ecosystem provision (Gate 3) — three analytically distinct properties that a single threshold cannot discriminate.

Key Design Decisions:

  1. Hypothetical withdrawal, not observed disruption. The severance test uses a hypothetical scenario rather than waiting for actual supply-chain disruption. This follows Basel III stress testing methodology: the test assesses structural resilience, not historical experience. Waiting for actual ecosystem severance before classifying would provide accurate data only after catastrophic events.

  2. 24–36 month sustainability window. The test asks whether met dimensions would remain operationally sustainable within 24–36 months, not indefinitely. This window reflects the approximate timeline for emergency substitution of critical AI infrastructure inputs (alternative chip supply, equipment qualification, model retraining on different compute). A shorter window would over-credit actors with large existing inventories; a longer window would under-credit actors with genuine but time-limited domestic capability.

  3. Three capability categories with ≥2 spanning requirement. The hardware/platform/governance trichotomy prevents an actor from qualifying as a PAA based solely on regulatory authority and investment (governance dimensions) without any hardware or platform independence. An actor exercising extraterritorial regulation and directing sovereign investment but possessing no domestic accelerator design, fabrication, equipment, cloud compute, or frontier models is not an ecosystem anchor — it is a regulatory power operating within another actor’s technological ecosystem.

  4. Advanced Capability State as a distinct classification. Rather than treating the severance test as a simple pass/fail that leaves non-qualifying actors undifferentiated, the methodology introduces a distinct classification tier. This captures the analytically significant difference between an actor scoring 4/7 with ecosystem dependence (e.g., the EU, which fails the severance test due to Intel ownership and Cymer dependency) and an actor scoring 0/7 (e.g., UAE). The “advanced capability state” classification is informative for users assessing supply-chain permission risk.

  5. AI Infrastructure Keystone (AIK) as a distinct classification. The three-gate structure produces a classification tier between PAA and ACS: actors that score ≥3, pass the severance test (demonstrating genuine ecosystem independence), but hold no sustainable platform dimension (D4 or D5). These actors exercise structural chokepoint leverage within an ecosystem — through equipment monopolies, fabrication capacity, or governance authority — without providing the ecosystem substrate (compute or model development platforms) upon which other actors operate. The AIK concept may be inherently asymmetric: it characterises the distributed Western supply chain (where equipment, fabrication, and governance are dispersed across allied actors) rather than vertically integrated ecosystems (where a single actor controls the full stack by necessity). Establishing cases: Japan (4/7, D3/D4/D6/D7, severance PASS; D4 met at assessment date but degraded-but-sustained under severance due to US-designed accelerator dependency), South Korea (4/7, D2/D3/D6/D7, severance PASS), Netherlands (3/7, D3/D6/D7, severance PASS).

Evidence Basis: The gate structure addresses the case where actors accumulate capability breadth whilst remaining ecosystem-dependent — Japan at 4/7 is the defining example. Analysis of Japan’s dependency chain demonstrates that its met dimensions (equipment, compute, regulation, investment) include structural dependencies on US-ecosystem inputs (accelerator design, frontier models) — a clear case where capability breadth does not automatically imply ecosystem equivalence with the two PAAs. Basel III stress testing provides the strongest structural precedent for combining a quantitative threshold with a resilience assessment. The third gate (sustainable platform dimension) addresses actors that pass the severance test but hold no sustainable platform dimension (Japan, South Korea, Netherlands) — distinguishing chokepoint leverage from ecosystem anchoring. The AIK classification resolves this by distinguishing actors that shape supply chains from actors that provide the ecosystem substrate. The severance test is applied across seven actors with two distinct outcomes: PASS (US 7/7 sustainable, China D1/D5/D6/D7 sustainable with D2 and D4 degraded-but-sustained, Japan D3/D6/D7 sustainable with D4 degraded-but-sustained, South Korea D3/D6/D7 sustainable with D2 degraded-but-sustained, Netherlands D3/D6/D7 with D3 sustained under stress with multi-vector exposure from Cymer jurisdiction plus five additional US leverage vectors identified in FDPR sub-tier research) and FAIL (EU — D2 collapses on Intel ownership, D3 at structural risk from Cymer and broader US leverage; Taiwan — all three MET dimensions structurally coupled to US ecosystem). The severance test’s operational components — ecosystem inputs definition, per-PAA testing, deterrence principle, cascade boundary, and dimension sustainability classifications — are documented in §5.4.3–§5.4.7.

The three-gate structure addresses a genuine analytical gap in the parent framework, and the Basel III stress testing analogy provides strong methodological precedent for the approach. The severance test is applied across seven actors (US, China, Japan, South Korea, Netherlands, EU, Taiwan), producing two distinct outcomes (PASS: US, China, Japan, South Korea, Netherlands; FAIL: EU, Taiwan) that are internally consistent and analytically meaningful. Confidence is above 70% because: the structural analogy to Basel stress testing is well-grounded; the underlying theoretical basis (Farrell & Newman’s weaponised interdependence) is established at very high confidence; the seven-actor application has produced a distribution that discriminates meaningfully; and the alternative (single- or dual-gate structures) produces demonstrably less useful classifications.

5.4.3 Ecosystem Inputs Definition

Manual Section: §5.3.1 (Severance Test Criteria — “Ecosystem inputs defined”)

Category: CATEGORY CREATOR refined (inherits 70–75% from §5.4.2)

Rationale: The ecosystem inputs definition uses a two-part (a)/(b) structure to draw a precise boundary around what the severance test withdraws: (a) inputs directly produced or controlled by entities under the PAA’s sovereign jurisdiction, and (b) inputs produced by entities outside the PAA’s jurisdiction but subject to denial through the PAA’s extraterritorial regulatory instruments (FDPR, direct EAR jurisdiction). This boundary follows the PAA’s sovereign denial capability — its ability to prevent an input from reaching the actor under test — rather than its manufacturing footprint or its geopolitical bloc membership. The China severance assessment established this boundary: Korean HBM blocked by FDPR is treated as a US ecosystem input (the US controls access through FDPR), whilst Zeiss and TRUMPF (both German and Western Bloc members) are treated as unaffected by US withdrawal (the US cannot deny their output).

The definition explicitly excludes bloc-wide withdrawal — “The severance test withdraws ecosystem inputs as defined above; it does not withdraw all inputs originating from actors aligned to the PAA’s geopolitical bloc” — because ecosystem inputs and bloc membership are distinct concepts. An actor can be in the same geopolitical bloc as the PAA without its inputs being subject to the PAA’s denial capability. The boundary is drawn at what the PAA can deny, not at who the PAA is aligned with.

The (b) formulation including direct EAR jurisdiction was supported by the FDPR sub-tier research (May 2026), which found that ASM International’s epitaxy product line is designed at ASM America in Phoenix, AZ — making it subject to direct EAR jurisdiction, not merely FDPR-eligible. The definition’s reference to “inputs subject to direct Export Administration Regulations jurisdiction from US-based design or manufacturing activity” captures this finding.

Key Design Decisions:

  1. Boundary drawn at denial capability, not production. A non-PAA actor’s inputs blocked by the PAA’s extraterritorial instruments (e.g., Korean HBM blocked by FDPR) are PAA ecosystem inputs because the PAA controls access to them. Inputs from the same non-PAA actor that are not blocked remain available. The boundary follows the PAA’s sovereign reach, not its manufacturing footprint.

  2. Explicit bloc exclusion. The final sentence of the definition — “it does not withdraw all inputs originating from actors aligned to the PAA’s geopolitical bloc” — is operationally necessary because without it, an assessor could conflate ecosystem withdrawal with bloc withdrawal, producing trivially incorrect FAIL verdicts for every Western actor.

Evidence Basis: China severance assessment (FDPR ruling on Korean HBM and ASML equipment); Netherlands severance assessment (Zeiss/TRUMPF treated as non-US, unaffected — establishing the ecosystem ≠ bloc boundary); FDPR sub-tier multi-agent AI research synthesis (9 entities across Japan, South Korea, Netherlands; May 2026) confirming the ASM International direct-EAR finding.

Establishing Cases: - China D1/D2: Korean HBM blocked by FDPR → US ecosystem input (withdrawn under severance) - Netherlands D3: Zeiss (German) and TRUMPF (German) → not US ecosystem inputs (available under US severance, despite being Western Bloc members) - Netherlands D3: ASM International epitaxy line designed at ASM America, Phoenix → direct EAR jurisdiction (US ecosystem input regardless of de minimis threshold)

Confidence Assessment: Inherits 70–75% from §5.4.2. The (a)/(b) boundary is grounded in the PAA’s sovereign denial capability — a precise, testable criterion supported by the FDPR sub-tier research confirming direct EAR jurisdiction findings.

Validation Commitment: Review if new extraterritorial regulatory instruments emerge beyond FDPR/EAR. Monitor for cases where the ecosystem/bloc boundary produces anomalous results. Reassess if a third PAA emerges, as the definition’s applicability to non-US extraterritorial instruments would need testing.

5.4.4 Per-PAA Severance Testing (Option B)

Manual Section: §5.3.1 (Severance Test Criteria — per-PAA testing)

Category: CATEGORY CREATOR refined (inherits 70–75% from §5.4.2)

Rationale: The choice of individual-PAA testing (each PAA tested separately, actor must pass each) over simultaneous-PAA testing (all PAAs withdrawn at once) follows established institutional practice for structural dependency assessment. Individual-scenario testing is the institutional default for classification and certification across financial, infrastructure, and supply-chain frameworks. Combined worst-case scenarios are reserved for non-binding strategic planning and resilience exercises. The methodological principle is that single-source dependency is a structural property of the assessed actor, whilst systemic fragility is a property of the global environment. Conflating them in one test produces a result that cannot be attributed to either cause, destroying the diagnostic value that classification is designed to provide. The severance test asks whether an actor’s capability stack is structurally contingent on another actor’s permission — a question that requires isolating each potential source of contingency.

5.4.5 Deterrence Principle

Manual Section: §5.3.1 (Severance Test Criteria — “Deterrence principle”)

Category: CATEGORY CREATOR refined (inherits 70–75% from §5.4.2)

Rationale: The deterrence principle classifies a dependency as “sustained under stress” rather than “collapsed” when the withdrawn PAA holds jurisdiction over a subsidiary or facility whose denial would be self-destructive for the PAA. This addresses the analytical problem that some dependencies are theoretically severable but practically unseverable because the PAA cannot exercise its denial capability without harming its own infrastructure capability. The three-part documentation requirement ensures assessor consistency by requiring explicit evidence for each deterrence claim.

Key Design Decisions:

  1. Three-part documentation requirement. Each deterrence assessment must document: (a) the specific facility at risk with jurisdictional basis, (b) the denial mechanism, (c) the specific cost to the PAA. The requirement ensures consistency across assessments — the Cymer dependency affects both the Netherlands and the EU, requiring explicit documentation of why deterrence applies differently for each (the Netherlands holds direct sovereign jurisdiction over ASML; the EU does not). The three-part structure makes each assessment explicit and auditable.

  2. Multi-vector leverage assessment. The FDPR sub-tier research (May 2026) revealed that the US holds five independent leverage vectors over Netherlands D3 beyond Cymer: ASML Wilton reticle top module (confirmed physical hardware), Brion computational lithography software (US-developed), HMI e-beam R&D, ASM International epitaxy line (direct EAR jurisdiction), and Besi Kinex cluster (Applied Materials co-development). Multiple leverage points weaken the deterrence argument because the PAA can apply graduated pressure through secondary vectors without triggering the self-harming consequences of disrupting the primary entity. The “sustained under stress with multi-vector exposure” classification captures this narrower margin of sustainability.

  3. Supranational entity clause. Deterrence based on a member state’s sovereign leverage over a headquartered entity does not automatically transfer to the supranational level. ASML is headquartered in the Netherlands (an EU member state), but the EU as a supranational entity does not hold the direct corporate-law jurisdiction, nationalisation authority, or operational sovereignty over ASML that the Netherlands holds as ASML’s home state. The EU can regulate ASML (competition law, Foreign Subsidies Regulation) but cannot ensure ASML’s continued operation against PAA pressure in the way the Netherlands can. The clause ensures that each actor’s deterrence assessment is based on its own sovereign jurisdiction over the relevant entity, producing the correct result: deterrence applies for the Netherlands (home state) but not for the EU (supranational entity).

Establishing Cases: - Netherlands D3 / Cymer: deterrence applied → “sustained under stress with multi-vector exposure” (five additional US leverage vectors beyond Cymer) - EU D3 / Cymer: deterrence NOT applied → “at structural risk” (supranational entity lacks direct sovereign jurisdiction over ASML; EU does not satisfy the supranational entity clause)

Evidence Basis: Netherlands severance assessment (Cymer deterrence precedent); EU severance assessment (supranational entity exclusion precedent); FDPR sub-tier multi-agent AI research synthesis (five US leverage vectors beyond Cymer confirmed: Wilton reticle module, Brion software, HMI R&D, ASM International direct EAR, Besi/Applied Materials partnership).

Confidence Assessment: Inherits 70–75% from §5.4.2. The deterrence principle is grounded in standard deterrence logic (mutual assured destruction applied to supply-chain dependencies). The multi-vector extension and supranational entity clause are grounded in deterrence logic and supported by establishing cases.

Validation Commitment: Monitor for changes in PAA leverage vectors (e.g., if Cymer operations are relocated to Europe, if ASML reduces US-origin content). Review supranational entity clause if EU institutional authority over corporate operational decisions strengthens. Monitor for new deterrence cases in Batch 2 assessments.

5.4.6 Cascade Analysis Boundary

Manual Section: §5.3.1 (Severance Test Criteria — “Cascade analysis boundary”)

Category: CATEGORY CREATOR refined (inherits 70–75% from §5.4.2)

Rationale: The single-level cascade boundary limits the depth of dependency analysis in the severance test. The assessor checks whether the actor’s primary capability-sustaining entities directly depend on the withdrawn PAA’s ecosystem inputs. Sub-tier dependencies (e.g., whether a Japanese equipment company’s sub-components contain US-origin FPGAs) are documented as constraints but do not determine the severance verdict. This boundary balances analytical rigour with practical tractability: full sub-tier supply chain mapping for every entity in every MET dimension is intractable from public sources, whilst ignoring dependencies entirely would produce unreliable verdicts.

Key Design Decisions:

  1. Single-level analysis is sufficient. The FDPR sub-tier research (May 2026) supported the single-level standard by demonstrating that entity-level analysis — examining US-origin design centres, US-acquired IP, US-jurisdiction subsidiaries, and US-dominated subsystem categories — identified the structural exposure patterns that determine severance verdicts. The research identified material findings at the single-level (ASM International direct EAR jurisdiction, ASML Wilton reticle module, Hanmi HIGH FDPR exposure) without requiring intractable component-level BOM analysis.

  2. Sub-tier findings documented as constraints, not verdicts. Sub-tier FDPR/EAR exposure is real but unverifiable from public sources at the component level. Incorporating unverifiable findings into the primary severance verdict would degrade the methodology’s evidence standard (§5.3.4). The constraint register captures the finding without degrading the methodology — creating a structured record for future investigation without changing current designations.

  3. Single-level boundary extends to non-PAA supply chain assessment. When criterion 1 requires assessing whether non-PAA actors’ supply chains would remain functional under severance, the same single-level boundary applies: the assessor checks whether the non-PAA actor’s primary entities depend on the withdrawn PAA’s ecosystem inputs, without tracing sub-tier dependencies.

Evidence Basis: FDPR sub-tier multi-agent AI research synthesis (9 entities across Japan, South Korea, Netherlands). Entity-level (single-level) analysis identified: TEL MODERATE FDPR exposure, Lasertec LOW-MODERATE, SCREEN MODERATE-LOW, HPSP MODERATE, Hanmi HIGH, ASML HIGH (non-Cymer), ASM International HIGH (direct EAR), Besi HIGH (Kinex) / MODERATE (standalone). These findings were consequential for constraint documentation (South Korea and Netherlands flagged for review) without requiring sub-tier BOM analysis.

Confidence Assessment: Inherits 70–75% from §5.4.2. The single-level boundary is supported by the FDPR research demonstrating that material findings emerge at entity level.

Validation Commitment: Monitor whether sub-tier findings (documented as constraints) produce designation-changing evidence in future assessment cycles. If sub-tier FDPR exposure is confirmed to change any entity’s sustainability classification, reassess whether the single-level boundary should be tightened for specific tool categories.

5.4.7 Dimension Sustainability Taxonomy

Manual Section: §5.3.1 (Severance Test Criteria — “Dimension sustainability classifications”)

Category: CATEGORY CREATOR refined (inherits 70–75% from §5.4.2)

Rationale: The five-level dimension sustainability taxonomy provides a closed set of classifications for assessing each met dimension under each per-PAA severance scenario. The five levels — sustainable, sustained under stress, sustained under stress with multi-vector exposure, degraded-but-sustained, and at structural risk — capture the analytically significant gradations between full independence and structural collapse. The taxonomy includes an explicit counting rule: only the first four levels count towards the ≥3 threshold; “at structural risk” does not.

Key Design Decisions:

  1. Five levels rather than binary (pass/fail). A binary dimension-level classification (sustainable / not sustainable) would lose analytically significant information. The difference between “sustainable” (Japan D3 — no US dependency in equipment itself) and “sustained under stress with multi-vector exposure” (Netherlands D3 — five US leverage vectors but deterrence holds) is material for risk assessment, even though both count towards the ≥3 threshold. The taxonomy preserves this gradation.

  2. “Degraded-but-sustained” as a counting level. A dimension that degrades under severance but remains above the §5.3.2 qualification threshold counts towards the ≥3 criterion. This is analytically correct because the severance test asks whether the actor can sustain its capability stack, not whether it can sustain it without any degradation. China’s D2 (SMIC on installed DUV equipment, no new EUV access) degrades significantly but retains ≤7nm production within the assessment window — this is a meaningful capability, not an analytical fiction.

5.4.8 AI Infrastructure Keystone (AIK) Designation

Manual Section: §5.3.1 (Actor Designation Framework — Gate 3)

Category: ADAPTED (82% confidence, with synthesis elements)

Rationale: The AIK designation classifies nation-state actors scoring ≥3 dimensions, passing the per-PAA severance test, but holding no sustainable platform dimension (D4 Cloud AI Compute or D5 Frontier AI Model Development). These actors hold structural chokepoint leverage within an AI ecosystem without providing the ecosystem itself. The structural distinction between ecosystem-providing power and chokepoint-leveraging power is not novel to WEO — it has been independently theorised across international relations, ecology, industrial organisation, network science, and documented in historical cases. WEO’s contribution is the bounded application of this well-established structural distinction to sovereign AI infrastructure capability assessment, combined with operational calibration (≥3/7 dimensional threshold, 24–36 month severance test, Gate 3 platform-dimension test).

The designation was supported by cross-disciplinary deep research across four independent AI research inputs with dedicated synthesis (May 2026), surveying frameworks across five domains. Eight independent intellectual traditions converge on essentially the same bifurcation between ecosystem-providing/dominant/hub/platform power and chokepoint-leveraging/keystone/bridge/bottleneck power, suggesting the distinction tracks a real structural feature of complex networked systems.

  2. Rey & Tirole (2007) as primary theoretical anchor.   3. PAA/AIK as ideal-type endpoints, not mutually exclusive categories. All five researched domains warn that ecosystem-provision and chokepoint-leverage co-occur in many actors.   4. Iansiti-Levien disambiguation required. Iansiti & Levien’s The Keystone Advantage (2004) uses “keystone” for platform hub orchestrators (Microsoft, Walmart) — aligning with PAA, not AIK. Manual §5.3.1 includes the disambiguation parenthetical.

  5. AIK fragility acknowledged as structural feature, not classification defect. Historical precedent demonstrates non-hegemonic chokepoint power erodes through substitution (OPEC post-1980), hegemonic coercion (Switzerland post-2008), or technological bypass (canal states). The 24–36 month severance test incorporates temporal bounds. AIK status is inherently provisional and subject to reassessment at each methodology version milestone, distinguishing it from PAA status which is more structurally durable. AIK leverage in the semiconductor supply chain is structurally deeper than commodity leverage — protected by cumulative process knowledge, IP portfolios, and capital barriers measured in decades — but it is not permanent.

  6. Österblom operational criteria adopted for AIK identification. To inoculate against the keystone-concept vagueness critique (Mills, Soulé & Doak 1993; Cottee-Jones & Whittaker 2012), the designation adopts Österblom et al.’s (2015) four-criteria approach adapted for AI infrastructure: (i) production concentration, (ii) segment control, (iii) network reach, (iv) governance influence.

  7. Formalising the non-hegemonic chokepoint gap in weaponised-interdependence theory. Farrell & Newman (2019) identified chokepoint and panopticon effects but located both in the same hegemon (US), under-theorising the case of small states hosting non-substitutable chokepoints within a hegemon’s broader ecosystem. WEO separates hegemon-hosted chokepoints from small-state-hosted chokepoints that F&N gesture at but do not formalise.

Category: WEO SYNTHESIS — Domain categories follow established sectoral frameworks; durability ranges derive from empirical sources (IRS depreciation, NRC licensing, insurance tenors, WTO principles); durability-based tiebreaker hierarchy is WEO-original, operationalising implicit market judgements and path dependence theory into explicit classification methodology.

Rationale: The four domains capture distinct coordination dynamics with materially different durability profiles:

Domain Durability Empirical Basis
Energy/Infrastructure 20-60 years Infrastructure asset lifespan data (IRS depreciation schedules, NRC licensing, TSMC operational evidence and industry analysis)
Security 15+ years effective horizon Institutionalised alliance persistence; political risk insurance tenors (MIGA 20-year maximum; DFC political risk insurance up to 20 years); implementing agreement cycles (DCAs, arms control)
Technology 2-5 years Technology lifecycle analysis; Moore’s Law adaptation cycles
Trade 3-10 years Trade agreement lifecycle data (WTO, bilateral FTAs)

Domain Durability Clarification

Technology Domain uses two overlapping ranges:

Context Range Typical Events
General classification 2-5 years Standards, protocols, algorithms
AI Software events 2-7 years Model releases, platforms, APIs

Rationale: AI software durability is more variable than general technology standards. Model releases (e.g., GPT-4 → GPT-5) have shorter lifecycles than protocol standards, whilst foundational AI platforms may persist longer.

Edge Cases: Foundational protocols (TCP/IP: 44+ years, IPv4: 40+ years) achieved durability through network effects, not design intent. These are exceptional and do not change typical domain classification. Original designers expected replacement.

Security Domain Durability Clarification

Security durability reflects effective horizon rather than formal treaty terms. Framework alliances (NATO, US-Japan, US-ROK) are formally perpetual with one-year withdrawal provisions, but institutionalised alliances create substantial practical lock-in through reputational costs, infrastructure entanglement, and institutional path dependence.

The “15+ years effective horizon” framing derives from convergent evidence:

Source Finding Implication
Political risk insurance MIGA offers 20-year maximum coverage; DFC political risk insurance up to 20 years Institutional investor consensus on reliable forecasting horizon for security commitments
Historical alliance persistence Major post-WWII alliances (NATO, US bilateral treaties) have persisted 70+ years Institutionalised alliances substantially exceed formal term structures
ATOP dataset Historical bilateral alliance duration data (1815-2018, v5.1) Historical volatility confirms alliances are not structurally perpetual
Withdrawal precedents No NATO member has formally exited in 75 years; partial exits (France 1966-2009) and reversal of withdrawal attempts (Philippines VFA 2020-2021) confirm exit remains practically possible High barriers but not absolute lock-in

Hierarchy Rationale: Security ranks below Energy/Infrastructure because the lock-in mechanisms differ fundamentally. Physical infrastructure creates irreversible sunk costs — a data centre, fab, or power plant cannot be “un-built.” Security commitments create substantial but reversible reputational and legal sunk costs — a determined government can exit an alliance, however costly, whereas it cannot dismantle physical infrastructure retroactively. This irreversibility asymmetry, rather than formal duration, justifies the domain hierarchy.

6.3 Domain Tiebreaker Hierarchy

Manual Section: 6.7

Category: WEO-ORIGINAL (derivative of durability ranges)

Rationale: The tiebreaker hierarchy follows durability order — domains creating longer-lasting structural commitments take classification precedence in genuinely ambiguous cases:

  1. Energy/Infrastructure (20-60 years) — Physical buildouts create highest-durability coordination
  2. Security (15+ years effective horizon) — Treaties and alliances persist across political cycles
  3. Technology (2-5 years) — Algorithmic/capability events take precedence over economic exchange
  4. Trade (3-10 years) — Economic agreements

Derivation: This hierarchy is derivative of the empirically-grounded Domain Durability Ranges (Manual Section 6.6).

Note: True 50/50 splits are rare. Most events have identifiable primary domains; tiebreaker application is exceptional.

Technology > Trade Ordering Rationale:

Technology ranks above Trade in the tiebreaker hierarchy despite shorter nominal durability (2-5 years vs 3-10 years) based on mechanism analysis. Technology standards and capability events exhibit stronger path dependence and network effects than trade agreements (Arthur, 1989; David, 1985). Technology choices create self-reinforcing lock-in through increasing returns and switching costs. Trade agreements, by contrast, include explicit modification provisions and demonstrate higher practical reversibility.

Citations: - Arthur, W. B. (1989). “Competing Technologies, Increasing Returns, and Lock-In by Historical Events.” The Economic Journal, 99(394), 116-131. - David, P. A. (1985). “Clio and the Economics of QWERTY.” American Economic Review, 75(2), 332-337.

Manual Section: 7.4, 7.7

Category: ADAPTED (MEDIUM-HIGH)

Rationale: Each event receives one primary tag (industry whose operational landscape the event’s documented provisions most directly address) and up to two secondary tags (industries whose operational landscapes are also identifiably addressed by documented provisions). This structure adapts well-established primary/secondary classification practice from multiple domains.

Evidence Chain:

7.5 Cross-Sector Transmission Frequency (CSTF)

Manual Section: 7.9 (quality standards context). Operationally specified in Towards Coordination Science §III.3; rationale grounded independently below via E-I Index equivalence

Category: ADAPTED (HIGH)

Rationale: When a Coordination Connection links two events with different primary industry tags, that CC represents a cross-sector transmission. CSTF measures the proportion of such cross-sector CCs:

CSTF = Cross-industry CCs ÷ Total CCs

This metric is mathematically near-identical to two established network metrics. It is a linear rescaling of the E-I Index (Krackhardt & Stern, 1988, Social Psychology Quarterly) and the complement of the diagonal trace of Newman’s mixing matrix (2003, Physical Review E, 67, 026126):

Established Metric Relationship to CSTF
E-I Index = (E − I) / (E + I) CSTF = (E-I + 1) / 2
Newman mixing matrix Tr(e) CSTF = 1 − Tr(e)

Both the E-I Index and Newman’s mixing matrix are implemented in standard network analysis packages (UCINET, igraph). When a metric is a linear transform of a 1988 publication and the complement of a 2003 formalism, it is adapted — not novel.

Evidence Chain:

Precedent Type Contribution
Krackhardt & Stern (1988, Social Psychology Quarterly) — E-I Index Direct (mathematical equivalence) CSTF is a linear rescaling; both measure external-to-total ratio for categorical network partitions
Newman (2003, Physical Review E, 67, 026126) — mixing matrix Direct (mathematical equivalence) CSTF = 1 − Tr(e); modularity Q provides null-model-corrected version
Diebold & Yilmaz (2012) — spillover index Analogue Cross-sector transmission ratios using continuous economic flows
Leontief Input-Output framework Analogue Inter-industry flow measurement; technical coefficients for cross-sector transmission
Guimerà & Amaral (2005, Nature) — participation coefficient Analogue Same concept at node level rather than network level

Novel Portion: ~25% — primarily naming (“CSTF”) and domain application (geopolitical AI infrastructure coordination events with typed CCs and industry tags). No published work applies cross-category network metrics to this domain.

Known Gap: CSTF omits the null-model baseline correction that both Newman (2003) and Krackhardt & Stern (1988) emphasise. Without this correction, a high CSTF could result from unequal group sizes (more events tagged “Semiconductors” than “Quantum Computing Infrastructure”) rather than genuine cross-sector coordination. The rescaling choice (CSTF’s 0–1 range vs. E-I’s −1 to +1) is justified for the target audience — institutional investors and policy analysts are more familiar with proportion-based metrics.

Confidence Assessment: HIGH. ~75% grounded through demonstrated mathematical equivalence to established network metrics. The null-model gap is a genuine weakness relative to established E-I Index implementation, preventing a higher confidence level.

Validation Commitment: (1) Report expected CSTF under random tag assignment alongside observed values, enabling users to distinguish genuine cross-sector coordination from artefacts of unequal tag distribution. (2) Cite the E-I Index explicitly as the established precedent. (3) At operational threshold, assess whether CSTF values are sensitive to reasonable tagging alternatives.

8.3 Binary Qualification with Evidence-Basis Descriptor

Manual Section: §8.2

Category: ADAPTED (HIGH 75–90%)

Rationale: ENT qualification is binary (present/absent) with an evidence-basis descriptor (“Documented” or “Assessed”) adapting the IPCC’s confidence gating model. The IPCC establishes a hierarchy where confidence is prerequisite to probability estimation; the ENT design adapts this: binary qualification is the primary gate, with the descriptor providing a simplified confidence indicator.

“Documented” means qualifying evidence appears in the event’s source documentation or primary regulatory sources (standard for ENT-1, ENT-2, ENT-5). “Assessed” means the analyst applied authoritative third-party data to documented facility characteristics (standard for ENT-4, and ENT-3 where measurement relies on independent monitoring). The two-value system was chosen over three-level confidence to minimise cognitive overhead for a supplementary metadata layer, consistent with ICD 203 permitting differentiated confidence architectures for supplementary assessments.

Why not three-level confidence? ENTs are supplementary metadata. Replicating the full CC confidence architecture (CC-V/CC-E/CC-A) would imply equivalent analytical weight. The evidence-basis descriptor captures the most analytically significant variation without over-engineering the confidence layer.

ICD 203 (ODNI, January 2015, Section D.6.e.(2)) Structural Differentiated confidence architectures permitted for supplementary assessments

8.4 Architectural Guardrails

Manual Section: §8.2

Rationale: Four guardrails ensure ENTs function as a supplementary enrichment layer without becoming a competing classification system.

8.5 ENT-1 Resource Demand — Definition and Threshold

Manual Section: §8.3.1

Category: EMPIRICALLY GROUNDED (HIGH 75–90%)

Rationale: The ≥100MW energy threshold derives from convergent institutional classification of “hyperscale” power demand. The International Energy Agency characterises large hyperscale data centres as those with capacity of 100MW or more (IEA, 2024; IEA, Energy and AI, April 2025); the United States Congressional Research Service uses the same characterisation (CRS R48646, August 2025), attributed to industry analysts; industry analysts (Synergy Research Group, Uptime Institute) converge on 100MW as the hyperscale classification boundary.

The threshold captures a qualitative shift: facilities exceeding 100MW typically interconnect directly to high-voltage transmission grid (115–230kV), a structural marker of environmental significance. Two alternative energy qualifiers (documented grid impact assessment, documented power purchase agreement) provide context-sensitive qualification below 100MW.

The ≥100MW energy threshold prevents trivial universality — without it, virtually every physical AI infrastructure event would qualify via the energy vector. The water, land, and minerals vectors do not need numerical thresholds because regulatory and planning systems determine what is environmentally significant enough to require dedicated documentation, providing a self-calibrating gate.

Non-Western validation confirms no jurisdiction uses 100MW as a regulatory boundary — regulatory triggers operate at lower levels (South Korea 10MW, Indonesia ~5MW equivalent, Japan energy-consumption-based). The threshold validates through industry convergence, not regulatory adoption, correctly identifying hyperscale environmental significance regardless of the host jurisdiction’s classification approach.

Why 100MW rather than 50MW or 200MW? 50MW risks trivial universality at scale. 200MW would exclude lower-range hyperscale and sovereign compute. 100MW has the strongest convergent institutional anchoring.

Type 6: Framework Family — Evidence Pathway Grounding

Manual Section: 9.2.1

Category: WEO SYNTHESIS

Rationale: The three Type 6 evidence pathways (common numbering, explicit cross-reference, parent framework document) combine three independently established concepts from standards management and legal instrument classification into a single evidence framework for geopolitical event relationship classification. Each pathway has an identified external basis; the combination and application to WEO’s domain is novel.

Research basis: A targeted survey of standards relationship classification systems was conducted across ISO/IEC Directives (Parts 1 and 2), NISO Standards Tag Suite (ANSI/NISO Z39.102), ISO Online Browsing Platform relationship categories, and national standards body concordance systems (BSI, ANSI, DIN, CEN/CENELEC).

Key findings:

  1. ISO/IEC does not formally define “standards family.” The only formally defined structural concept is the multipart standard (ISO/IEC Directives, Part 2, Clause 5.1). ISO’s colloquial use of “family” (e.g., “ISO 27000 family”) is defined by number range, not committee membership. No surveyed standards body provides a normative definition.
  2. The normative reference is the only relationship type creating binding technical dependency. ISO/IEC Directives Part 2 defines normative references as documents “referred to in the text in such a way that some or all of their content constitutes requirements of this document.” Informative references (bibliography, body text, informative annexes) provide contextual information without imposing requirements.
  3. Committee membership is administrative metadata, not an inter-standard relationship. The NISO STS schema classifies inter-standard relationships through the <std-xref> element — relationship types include “revises,” “supersedes,” “amends,” “adopted-from,” and “std-family.” Committee attribution and ICS classification appear as metadata but do not constitute formal inter-standard relationships.
  4. Cross-body adoption creates no transitive relationships. The ISO-IEEE PSDO agreement (2007) defines fast-track adoption and joint development mechanisms. Adoption renumbers a standard into the adopting body’s catalogue but creates no normative links to other standards in that catalogue.

Chinese Regulatory Temporal Accommodation

Manual Section: §9.4.1 (Temporal Windows)

Category: ADAPTED (78–85% confidence)

Rationale: The 根据 clause in Article 1 of a Chinese departmental regulation is a mandatory drafting feature established by the Regulations on Procedures for the Formulation of Rules (《规章制定程序条例》, State Council Order No. 322, revised 2017). The NPCSC Legislative Technical Standards (Trial) (No. 2) (法工委发 [2011] 5号, §13) draws an explicit distinction: 根据 is for citing the Constitution or a parent statute as a legislative basis in Article 1, whilst 依据 (yījù) is for citing specific clauses in operative provisions. This confirms that 根据 establishes an authority dependency — analogous to US CFR authority citations (1 CFR §21.40–.43) or EU “Having regard to” preambles — rather than a content dependency analogous to ISO/IEC Clause 2 normative references.

Chinese regulatory families constructed through 根据 chains exhibit 24–42 month spans between parent and child instruments. The CAC AI regulatory family demonstrates this pattern: Algorithm Recommendation Provisions (March 2022) → Deep Synthesis Provisions (January 2023, ~10 months) → Generative AI Measures (August 2023, ~17 months) → AI Content Labelling Measures (September 2025, ~42 months from parent). Comparative data confirms these timescales are normal: EU Level-1 financial-services acts average 25 months from proposal to publication with a range extending to 51 months (Clifford Chance, February 2025); US rulemaking under enabling statutes commonly takes 3–5 years.

The accommodation resolves a routing asymmetry identified through multi-source research (unanimous convergence across independent inputs). The same functional relationship — a parent authority instrument and a child implementing regulation separated by a multi-year gap — is classified as different CC types depending on jurisdiction. Western authority chains (e.g., an enabling statute and the agency rule issued under it) typically qualify as Type 6 (Framework Family) through Pathway 3, which has no temporal window, or as Type 1 (Policy Cascade), which has longer windows (up to 60 months for Energy/Infrastructure). Chinese 根据 chains are classified as Type 4 (Regulatory Enablement) — the child regulation operates within the framework the parent created — where the Technology domain window is 24 months. The result is that functionally equivalent authority chains pass or fail temporal qualification based on which CC type they are routed through, not on the strength of the documented relationship.

The three-constraint design (explicit full-title citation, CC-V grade, 48-month bound) ensures the accommodation is narrow: it applies only to formally documented authority chains, not to thematic proximity or informal policy influence.

The FATF Mutual Evaluation Methodology provides the strongest precedent for legal-tradition accommodation, requiring assessors to account for “the nature of the legal system (civil law or common law) and institutional framework.” GDPR adequacy assessments apply functional equivalence rather than formal identity (Schrems II, Case C-311/18, ¶105).

Threshold Derivation — Empirical Basis

Academic Foundation:

The policy diffusion literature establishes that parallel policy adoption occurs through four mechanisms (Shipan & Volden, 2008):

Mechanism Description Observable Indicators
Learning Jurisdictions observe and adopt successful policies from others Similar policy design, temporal sequence
Competition Jurisdictions adopt policies to maintain competitive position Similar targets, similar timing
Imitation Jurisdictions copy policies for legitimacy without deep analysis High mechanism similarity, rapid adoption
Coercion External pressure drives adoption Explicit conditionality, power asymmetry

All four mechanisms produce observable similarity across the same dimensions: mechanism, target, scope, and timing. The academic literature does not specify quantitative thresholds for “sufficient similarity” — this is the gap WEO fills.

Threshold Calibration Analysis:

Research on policy diffusion patterns (Berry & Berry, 1990; Gilardi & Wasserfallen, 2019) suggests:

Similarity Level Observed Pattern Interpretation
4/4 criteria Rare (<5% of policy pairs) Strong indicator of explicit coordination; may warrant Joint Issuance investigation
3/4 criteria Uncommon (10-20% of policy pairs) Consistent with diffusion mechanisms; sufficient for Parallel Policy classification
2/4 criteria Common (30-40% of policy pairs) Likely coincidental; policies in same domain often share some characteristics
1/4 or 0/4 criteria Very common (>50% of policy pairs) No meaningful similarity

WEO Calibration Decision:

The 3-of-4 threshold was selected based on:

  1. Distinctiveness: 3/4 similarity is uncommon enough (~10-20% of policy pairs) to indicate meaningful connection rather than coincidence
  2. Observability: All four criteria are objectively assessable from policy documentation
  3. Conservatism: Setting threshold at 3/4 rather than 2/4 reduces false positives whilst accepting some false negatives (missed connections)
  4. Analytical designation: Parallel Policy is the only CC type permitting CC-A (Analytical) confidence, explicitly acknowledging the inferential nature of similarity-based connections

Across 63 Parallel Policy CCs at V1.4 publication, all 22 assessments lacking quantitative data returned Met, consistent with the expectation that events passing mechanism and target similarity at the same jurisdictional level are inherently scope-comparable.

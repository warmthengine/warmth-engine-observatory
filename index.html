The C-MAD Framework

Computational Mutual Assured Destruction (C-MAD) examines how operational AI infrastructure concentration creates coordination pressure through mutual vulnerability. Unlike development-phase frameworks addressing pre-deployment competition, C-MAD analyses strategic dynamics once systems are already integrated into critical infrastructure.

When financial systems, logistics networks, communications platforms, and energy grids depend on concentrated processing capacity, disruption costs exceed competitive benefits for all parties simultaneously. This transforms strategic incentives from zero-sum competition towards coordination pressure.

C-MAD vs MAIM: Critical Distinction

MAIM (Mutual Assured AI Malfunction): Addresses pre-deployment phase through deterrence - preventing dangerous AI development via credible sabotage threats targeting adversaries' computational infrastructure.

C-MAD (Computational Mutual Assured Destruction): Addresses operational phase through coordination - managing existing dependencies once AI systems are already integrated into critical infrastructure. When systems are operational, disruption harms all parties simultaneously, creating Stag Hunt coordination dynamics rather than Prisoner's Dilemma defection incentives.

The Four-Headed Analysis

Complex systems operate through interconnected domains maintaining functional independence whilst contributing to systemic objectives:

  • Technological Head: Infrastructure development and hardware systems
  • Financial Head: Resource allocation and value capture mechanisms
  • Psychological Head: Behavioural conditioning and attention dynamics
  • Governance Head: Regulatory frameworks and authorisation processes

WE GSI Dimensional Framework

The WE GSI uses exactly 5 dimensions to operationalize the Four-Headed Analysis:

Dimension 1: Technological Head Coordination (-2 to +2)

Definition: The degree to which AI infrastructure - compute capacity, data centres, supply chains, and energy systems - is shared and interoperable versus fragmented and sovereign.

Scoring Criteria:

  • +2 (Strong Coordination): Joint infrastructure development, shared compute resources, coordinated supply chain diversification. Example: Multi-country AI data centre consortiums, cross-border power purchase agreements for AI infrastructure.
  • +1 (Moderate Coordination): Compatible systems without explicit integration, friend-shoring strategies, regional cooperation. Example: Cloud platforms operating across allied nations with compatible architecture.
  • 0 (Neutral/Ambiguous): Parallel infrastructure development without explicit coordination or restriction. Example: Domestic data centre builds without cross-border linkages.
  • -1 (Moderate Fragmentation): Preferential domestic infrastructure, limited interoperability, supply chain bifurcation. Example: Sovereign cloud mandates, behind-the-meter data centre power generation.
  • -2 (Strong Fragmentation): Explicit technological decoupling, adversarial infrastructure, complete supply chain separation. Example: Coordinated semiconductor export controls, mandatory indigenous technology stacks.

Dimension 2: Financial Head Coordination (-2 to +2)

Definition: The openness versus restriction of capital flows, investment, and financial resources for AI development.

Scoring Criteria:

  • +2 (Strong Coordination): Coordinated capital flow management, reciprocal investment frameworks, joint funding vehicles. Example: Allied nations establish matching venture co-investment funds for dual-use AI.
  • +1 (Moderate Coordination): Open investment with standard screening, bilateral investment agreements. Example: FDI screening exists but approvals common for non-adversarial investments.
  • 0 (Neutral/Ambiguous): General regulatory stability, no targeted AI investment policies. Example: Free capital flow with standard CFIUS review applying to all sectors equally.
  • -1 (Moderate Fragmentation): Selective investment restrictions, increased FDI scrutiny for AI, strategic sector protection. Example: Expanded CFIUS scope, domestic-priority subsidies.
  • -2 (Strong Fragmentation): Aggressive outbound and inbound investment controls, capital prohibitions, adversarial financial positioning. Example: Outbound investment rules prohibiting AI chip investment in adversarial nations, forced divestitures.

Dimension 3: Psychological Head Coordination (-2 to +2)

Definition: The trust, confidence, behavioural patterns, and narrative framing that enable or inhibit AI infrastructure cooperation.

Scoring Criteria:

  • +2 (Strong Coordination): Formalised trust infrastructure, mutual recognition agreements, shared ethical frameworks. Example: Harmonised ethical AI frameworks for defence applications, binding AI safety standards.
  • +1 (Moderate Coordination): Consistent positive framing, bilateral trust-building initiatives, voluntary ethical alignment. Example: G7 voluntary AI principles, bilateral AI cooperation workstreams.
  • 0 (Neutral/Ambiguous): Mixed public sentiment, unstable narratives, absence of clear trust signals. Example: Competing narratives without dominant frame.
  • -1 (Moderate Fragmentation): Adversarial framing, trust erosion, competing ethical frameworks. Example: Incompatible values-based AI content requirements, systematic trust gaps in data governance.
  • -2 (Strong Fragmentation): Weaponised narratives, systematic trust breakdown, adversarial behavioural conditioning. Example: AI-enabled malign influence operations, declared systemic distrust in official statements.

Dimension 4: Governance Head Coordination (-2 to +2)

Definition: The alignment versus divergence of policies, regulations, legal frameworks, and authorisation mechanisms governing AI development and deployment.

Scoring Criteria:

  • +2 (Strong Coordination): Unified regulatory frameworks, multilateral agreement implementation, harmonised standards. Example: Coordinated semiconductor export controls, international AI standards with mutual recognition.
  • +1 (Moderate Coordination): Bilateral policy alignment, compatible regulations, multilateral forum participation. Example: G7 AI processes, bilateral regulatory coordination frameworks.
  • 0 (Neutral/Ambiguous): Diverse policies without strong conflict, different approaches without incompatibility. Example: Different regulatory philosophies without direct clash.
  • -1 (Moderate Fragmentation): Conflicting regulations, incompatible compliance requirements, unilateral restrictions. Example: Conflicting data localisation requirements, proliferation of incompatible audit frameworks.
  • -2 (Strong Fragmentation): Adversarial policy frameworks, designed incompatibility, weaponised regulation. Example: Mandatory data localisation with extraterritorial enforcement, complete regulatory decoupling.

Dimension 5: Temporal Momentum (-1 to +1)

Definition: The recent directional change across all four APMF heads, capturing whether coordination dynamics are improving, stable, or deteriorating.

Scoring Criteria:

  • +1 (Positive Momentum): Recent announcements of new partnerships, easing of restrictions, trust-building initiatives. Example: New multilateral AI cooperation frameworks announced in past 90 days.
  • 0 (Stable): Continuation of existing patterns without major shifts. Example: Existing partnerships maintained without expansion or contraction.
  • -1 (Negative Momentum): Recent restrictions, partnership dissolutions, adversarial policy announcements. Example: New export controls implemented, investment bans announced.
